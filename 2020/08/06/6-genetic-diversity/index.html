Genetic diversity

Preface

Genetic diveristy describes the genetic differences among individuals within the same population. The higher genetic diveristy may imply the larger gene pool, and also indicate the higher stability.

Factors including genetic drift, natural selection, migration, gene flow, population admixture, bottleneck, and sexual selection may influence the genetic diveristy.

In the last ~10 thousand years, since the human dispersal out of Afria, our ancestors had experienced war, hunger, and all kinds of changing environment. They survived, the way out of Africa made them stronger. All of these experiences were recorded in our blood, in every nucleotide on our genome.

We have to know about ourselves, about our origin, also about our futher. The key to the futher is on our genome, passed from our ancestors. We are generating huge mount of genetic data and we are decoding the variations on our genome. We are amazed about the diversity of our genome.

Now, let’s focus on the etimators of the genetic diveristy.

DNA polymorphism

DNA polymorphisms indicates the variations on DNA sequences among individuals and populations. The forms of DNA polymorphisms include single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), variable number of tandem repeats (VNTRs, e.g., minisatellites), short tandom repeat (STR, e.g., microsatellite), copy number variation (CNV), and so on.

SNPs are the most common type of genetic variations in humans (90% of all human DNA polymorphism). A SNP is a substitution of a single nucleotide at a specific position in the genome, that is present in a sufficiently large fraction of the population (e.g. 1% or more). And a single-nucleotide variant (SNV) is a variation in a single nucleotide without any limitations of frequency (which should be minor allele frequency (MAF) >1%).

There has been identified millions of comman SNPs among human populations (MAF >5%) (roughly, say 5~10 million). It implies ~0.1% difference between every two humans. P.S. it should be ~1% between human and Chimpanzee.

Heterozygosity

The fraction of individuals in a population that are heterozygous for a particular locus.

Observed: $H_o=\frac{\sum_{1}^{n}(1 \ if \ a_{i1}\ne a_{i2})}{n}$

where n is the number of individuals in the population, and $a_{i1}$, $a_{i2}$ are the alleles of individual i at the target locus.

Expected: $H_e=1-\sum_{1}^{m}(f_i)^2$

where m is the number of alleles at the target locus, and $f_i$ is the allele frequency of the $i^{th}$ allele at the target locus.

High heterozygosity means lots of genetic variability. Low heterozygosity means little genetic variability, which may also indicate severe effects of small population sizes (e.g., population bottlenecks).

Often, we will compare the observed level of heterozygosity to what we expect under Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE). If the observed heterozygosity is lower than expected, we seek to attribute the discrepancy to forces such as inbreeding. If heterozygosity is higher than expected, we might suspect an isolate-breaking effect (the mixing of two previously isolated populations).

HWE: $p^2+2pq+q^2=1$ under the Wright-Fisher Model

Wright-Fisher model:

  • Infinite population size
  • Non-overlapping generation
  • Random mating
  • No natural selection
  • …

Population mutation rate ($\theta$)

In population genetics, population mutation rate ($\theta$) describe the genetic diversity in a population.

  • $\theta=4N_e\mu$ for autosome
  • $\theta=N_e\mu$ for Y and mtDNA
  • $\theta=3N_e\mu$ for X chromosome

where $N_e$ is the effective population size and $\mu$ is the per-generation mutation rate of the population of interest

see the Effective population size section for more details about these formulas

There are many estimators of $\theta$, including average pairwise differences (nucleotide diversity) ($\theta_\pi$), number of segregating sites ($\theta_K$), and so on.

Nucleotide diversity ($\theta_\pi$)

One commonly used measure of nucleotide diversity was first introduced by Nei and Li in 1979. This measure is defined as the average number of nucleotide differences per site between two DNA sequences in all possible pairs in the sample population, and is denoted by $\pi$.

It is given by the formula:

$\pi=\sum{x_ix_j\pi_{ij}}=2*\sum_{i=2}^{n}{\sum_{j=1}^{i-1}x_ix_j\pi_{ij}}$

where $x_i$ and $x_j$ are the respective frequencies of the $i^{th}$ and $j^{th}$ sequences, $\pi_{ij}$ is the number of nucleotide differences per nucleotide site between the $i^{th}$ and $j^{th}$ sequences, and $n$ is the number of sequences in the sample.

Nei, M., and Li, W.H. (1979). MATHEMATICAL-MODEL FOR STUDYING GENETIC-VARIATION IN TERMS OF RESTRICTION ENDONUCLEASES. PANS

There is an easier way to calculate $\pi$.
For a particular locus $i$ in one population, we have

$\pi_i=\frac{number \ of \ allele \ 1 \ * \ number \ of \ allele \ 2}{n*(n-1)/2}$

for example:

sequence 1: NNNNNNNNNNANNNNNNNNNN

sequence 2: NNNNNNNNNNCNNNNNNNNNN

sequence 3: NNNNNNNNNNANNNNNNNNNN

allele A count = 2, allele C count = 1

total pairwise difference(s) = $2*1$

number of alll possible pairs = $3*(3-1)/2$

$\pi=0.67$

then, $\pi=\sum_{}^{L}\pi_i$

where $n$ is the number of sequences, and $L$ is the total number of loci on the DNA sequence

which means we can easily calculate $\pi$ based on the summary data of allele frequency

this formula is only for biallelic loci. It’s also easy to conduct the formula for multiallelic loci.

In general, $\pi_{AFR}>\pi_{EUR}>\pi_{EAS}$, $\pi_{admixed}>\pi_{reference}$

Time to Most Recent Common Ancestor (TMRCA)

It’s a long story, starting from the very begining. God created the world as well ancestors of humans, Adam and Eve. Then, Adam and Eve created human beings.

OK, let’s talk about science. A lot of work have been done to build and improve the human Y chromosome and mitochondrial phylogenetic tree. Since mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) and Y chromosome are respectively inherited maternally and paternally, while recombination is rare or absent, it seems relatively easy to track the ancestry of the lineages back to a Most Recent Common Ancestor (MRCA). And scientists have helped to trace our lineages back to single person living in Africa, named Mitochondrial Eve and Y-chromosomal Adam. The principal is the coalescence of mutations on every pairs of sequences. Meanwhile, we are also able to calculate TMRCA by estimating the expected time to have all of the observed mutations occured on Y chromosome / mtDNA.

For autosome, there is also a simple way to estimate TMCRA. First, chose a short segment on the genome to avoid potential recombination. Then calculate the average pairwise differences among sequences (in the same way as $\pi$).

not necessarily sequences from the same population, depend on your purpose. you are able to estimate the within population TMRCA using sequences from the same population. or you can estimate the cross-population TMRCA (divergence time) using sequences from different populations.

formulas would be a little bit different

within-population:
$\pi=\sum{\frac{number \ of \ allele \ 1 \ * \ number \ of \ allele \ 2}{n*(n-1)/2}}$

cross-population:
$\pi=\sum{\frac{c_{1A} \ * \ c_{2B} \ + \ c_{2A} \ * \ c_{1B}}{n_A \ * \ n_B}}$

$c_{1A}$: number of allele 1 in population A
$c_{2A}$: number of allele 2 in population A
$c_{1B}$: number of allele 1 in population B
$c_{2B}$: number of allele 2 in population B

$TMRCA=\frac{\pi}{2 \mu L}$

where $\mu$ denotes the local mutation rate, and $L$ is the length of the regional segment.

$\mu=\frac{d_{human \ vs. \ human-ape \ ancestor}}{L \ * \ T_{human \ to \ human-ape \ ancestor}}$

then,

$TMRCA=\frac{T_{human \ to \ human-ape \ ancestor} \ * \ \pi}{d_{human \ vs. \ human-ape \ ancestor} \ * \ 2}$

$d_{human \ vs. \ human-ape \ ancestor}$ is the nucleotide difference of the target region between the human reference genome and that of the most recent common ancestor of humans-chimpanzee. ($\pi$ of 2 sequences)

$d_{human \ vs. \ human-ape \ ancestor}$ should be half the difference between the human reference genome and that of the chimpanzee

$T_{human \ to \ human-ape \ ancestor}$ is the divergence time of humans and chimpanzees. Here we use 13 million years.
